Cleaning Caddies questions, answered

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

A cleaning caddy keeps your supplies portable and tidy, while an under sink organizer maximizes awkward cabinet space. Choose based on your storage needs, preferred material (plastic, metal, or fabric), and whether you carry supplies room to room or store them in place.

What should I look for in a cleaning caddy?

Start with portability. If you carry supplies between rooms, look for a sturdy handle and a balanced shape that won’t tip. Compartments matter: a mix of deep wells for bottles and slots for brushes or sprayers keeps everything upright. Removable dividers or trays let you reconfigure as your collection changes. Next, consider material. Plastic caddies are lightweight and easy to rinse, but can crack if dropped with heavy loads. Metal or coated wire caddies feel sturdier and often drain better, though they may lack individual compartments. Fabric totes with stiffened bases collapse less than you’d think but absorb spills over time. Owner reviews consistently praise caddies with a non-slip base or rubber feet, so bottles don’t slide around.

Plastic vs. metal vs. fabric caddies: which is best?

Plastic caddies dominate the market because they’re cheap, lightweight, and easy to clean. Look for thick, food-grade polypropylene rather than flimsy recycled plastic, those crack near the handle after a few months. Many professional cleaners prefer the simple plastic box with a built-in handle; it’s nearly indestructible for household use. Metal caddies, often powder-coated steel or wire, offer longevity and a more polished look. They resist stains and odors better than plastic, but they rust if the coating chips. Wire caddies allow air circulation, which helps damp sponges dry out. The trade-off is weight: metal caddies are heavier when fully loaded. Fabric caddies, usually canvas or nylon with a waterproof liner, collapse or pack flat for storage. They work well for light, daily carry but struggle with heavy glass bottles. Owners report that zippers and seams fail first, so avoid fabric if you store acidic cleaners long-term.

How do I choose the right size caddy for my needs?

Think about what you’ll carry at once. A small caddy (roughly the size of a shoebox) fits a few sprays, a scrub brush, and a pack of cloths, ideal for a quick kitchen tidy or a bathroom counter. A medium caddy can hold a full cleaning kit: glass cleaner, all-purpose spray, a bottle of bleach, a window squeegee, and sponges. Large caddies, sometimes with a second tier or a shoulder strap, suit multi-room deep cleans. Measure your storage space before buying. Under sink cabinets vary widely, and a full-size caddy might not fit below the pipes. Also check handle height: if you store it on a shelf, the handle must clear the shelf above. Reviews often mention that a caddy is either “exactly the right size” or “way bigger than I expected,” so compare it to a common object like a cereal box or a milk jug.

What should I consider when buying an under sink organizer?

Measure your cabinet’s depth, width, and the height of the plumbing trap. Most organizers sit on the bottom shelf, but you can also get stackable bins or pull-out trays that fit around the pipes. A U-shaped or cutout design that wraps around the plumbing is best for awkward layouts. Adjustable shelves or tiered racks let you store cleaning bottles on one level and sponges or gloves below. Material matters here too. Under sink areas get humid; moisture-proof materials like coated steel or thick plastic resist warping. Avoid MDF or particleboard unless it’s sealed on all edges, swelling and mold are common complaints. Also check for non-slip feet or adhesive strips to keep the organizer from sliding when you open the door.

How can I keep my cleaning caddy organized long-term?

Pair your caddy with a routine. Every week or two, empty it, wipe down the interior, and toss expired or empty bottles. Group items by room: glass cleaner and microfiber cloths for the kitchen, a separate set with disinfectant and toilet cleaner for bathrooms. Use small mesh pouches or baby wipe packs for loose items like scrub brushes or grout sponges. Labeling helps, especially if multiple people use the caddy. Stick a small piece of washi tape or a label maker tag on each bottle’s top so you can grab the right one without digging. If your caddy doesn’t have compartments, add reusable silicone muffin cups or mini buckets to separate spray nozzles from cloths.

Are there caddies or organizers designed for specific cleaning styles?

Yes. Some caddies are built for natural cleaning enthusiasts, with compartments sized for vinegar, baking soda, and castile soap bottles. Others cater to minimalists who own exactly three spray bottles and a squeegee. For commercial or deep-cleaning fans, look for caddies that hold a bucket or incorporate a microfiber mop head within a secondary compartment. For under sink use, there are organizers specifically shaped to accommodate a trash can on one side and cleaning supplies on the other. Some come with pull-out shelves that allow you to reach items in the back without crawling on hands and knees. A few models even include built-in moisture absorbers or ventilation grilles to prevent mold.

Frequently asked questions

Can I use a cleaning caddy for other tasks besides cleaning?

Absolutely. Many people repurpose caddies as art supply carriers, kitchen utensil holders, gardening tool totes, or even travel toiletry organizers. Just make sure to rinse out any harsh chemical residue before switching uses.

How do I clean my cleaning caddy without damaging it?

For plastic caddies, a quick soak in warm, soapy water and a scrub with a soft brush works. Metal caddies should be dried immediately to prevent rust. Fabric caddies with removable liners can often be machine-washed on gentle; always check the care tag. Avoid bleach on metal or printed plastics.

What’s the best way to arrange bottles in a caddy so they don’t tip over?

Place the heaviest bottles (like bleach or floor cleaner) in the center, close to the handle. Spray bottles with long necks fit well in vertical slots or corner compartments. If your caddy lacks sections, roll a hand towel and tuck it between bottles to keep them upright during travel.

Should I get a single large caddy or multiple smaller ones?

It depends on your routine. A single large caddy works if you clean the whole house in one pass. Multiple smaller caddies let you assign one per room, kitchen, bathroom, home office, so you grab just what you need and avoid cross-contamination from toilets to dishrags.

Do under sink organizers come with installation or assembly required?

Many are ready to use out of the box: just place the rack or bin on the cabinet floor. Some pull-out drawer versions require sliding rails to be screwed into the cabinet sides. Check product descriptions and reviews to see if tools are needed, if you’re not handy, choose a no-assembly option.

Can I put an under sink organizer in a cabinet with a garbage disposal?

Yes, but you’ll need to measure carefully. The bulk of the disposal and drain pipes takes up most of the lower area. Look for slim, stackable bins that fit on the sides or behind the disposal. Some organizers are designed to straddle the trap, with a cutout in the back for pipe clearance.

In shortA good cleaning caddy or under sink organizer makes tidying up less of a chore and more of a system. Focus on materials that hold up to moisture, sizes that fit your home’s nooks, and compartments that match how you actually use your supplies. Browse owner reviews to see how your top picks hold up after six months of real-world use, and don’t be afraid to return one that doesn’t click with your routine. With the right caddy, you’ll spend less time hunting for supplies and more time checking rooms off your list.
